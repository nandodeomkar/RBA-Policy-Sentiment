Methodology

How these scores are built

Every score on this site comes from an offline batch job — never live, never hand-tuned per decision. This page documents what goes in, how the tone is measured, and where it can be wrong. Transparency is the point: a score you can't inspect isn't one you should trust.

The corpus

We score the RBA's monetary policy decision media releases only — the short statement published with each cash-rate decision. Not the meeting minutes, not the Statement on Monetary Policy, not speeches; and the Reserve Bank of Australia only, no other central banks.

The current dataset is 64 decisions, spanning February 2020 – May 2026 (42 holds, 16 hikes, 6 cuts).

We never store or republish the full statement text. We keep only short evidence phrases and link every score to the original release on rba.gov.au.

The ensemble

Three independent methods score each statement, then a reconciliation step combines them. They're deliberately different, so the amount they disagree is itself informative.

  • Lexicon

    A transparent finance / hawkish–dovish term list. Fully deterministic and inspectable — the same words always score the same way, with no model in the loop.

  • LLM

    A large-language-model sentence classifier (currently Claude Haiku). Its calls are pinned and cached, so re-scoring the same text returns byte-identical results.

  • Transformer

    A fine-tuned finance sentiment model. This slot currently runs an interim model trained on US Federal Reserve communications as a stand-in; an RBA-specific model is the planned replacement (see limitations).

Each component returns its own net score and inflation / growth / employment sub-scores; you can see all three side by side in the breakdown panel under any decision on the home page.

Reconciliation & confidence

The three results are combined into the published net score. The current method is an equal-weight mean of the three components.

Where the methods disagree, the published confidence falls — so confidence measures how much the methods agreed, not how sure any single one of them is. Low confidence is shown prominently in the breakdown, never hidden; it's the product's honesty signal.

The score scales

Net stance runs on a fixed, symmetric −1 … +1 scale — dovish below zero, hawkish above, zero broadly neutral. The axis is never truncated or rescaled to dramatise a result. The inflation, growth and employment sub-scores use the same scale, so they're directly comparable.

For plain-language labels, net scores fall into these buckets:

Net scoreLabel
≤ −0.60Strongly dovish
−0.60 to −0.15Dovish
−0.15 to +0.15Broadly neutral
+0.15 to +0.60Hawkish
≥ +0.60Strongly hawkish

Confidence is labelled high (≥ 0.80), moderate (0.50–0.80) or low (< 0.50). These thresholds are a documented judgement call — they're chosen, not derived — and live in one place in the code so they can be tuned without restructuring anything.

Reproducibility & provenance

Each score is computed once and persisted; the LLM calls are pinned and cached, so re-running the same text under the same engine version is byte-identical. Every score records the engine version that produced it, and every score links to its canonical rba.gov.au release.

Known limitations

  • Interim transformer. The transformer slot currently runs a model trained on US Federal Reserve text, not RBA statements. How well that transfers to Australian language is not yet validated; the component may be down-weighted or replaced.
  • Accuracy not yet independently validated. The scores have not yet passed the project's accuracy benchmark (≥ 85% agreement with an expert-labelled set). Until they do, treat the series as indicative and pre-launch — scores may change as the engine improves.
  • Thresholds are judgement calls. The dovish / neutral / hawkish cut-offs above are chosen, not derived from data.
  • Scope is narrow by design. Decision media releases only — no minutes, Statement on Monetary Policy, or speeches; no attribution of tone to individual board members; and no forecasting of future decisions.
  • Not financial advice. This is general information about the RBA's published words, produced by an independent project that is not affiliated with the RBA.

Get the data

The full scored dataset is downloadable as CSV — one row per decision, with date, outcome, net stance, the inflation / growth / employment sub-scores, confidence, engine version and source URL. For reproducibility, cite the engine version shown above.
